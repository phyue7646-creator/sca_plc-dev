# SCA Group Project Brief — AY2026/27 April Semester

## Format

- **Presentation:** 15–20 minutes presentation + 5 minutes Q&A
- **Group size:** Typically 3–6 students
- **Group leader:** At least one group leader must be appointed. The leader is responsible for updating work progress and member contributions to the tutor, as well as timely project submission. Group leaders are awarded higher tutorial participation marks for effective group management.

## Assessment Objectives

Through this Group Project, students will be able to learn-act-share about sustainability. In addition, they will:

- Collaborate as global citizens and future leaders to conduct credible research on a Climate Change challenge or Problem Statement.
- Propose innovative, out-of-the-box, creative solutions or social campaigns that help address the Climate Change challenge or Problem Statement.
- Showcase support for the Green Economy and Sustainability.

## Project Timeline

The project is guided by three consultations:

1. **Consultation 1:** Introduction & Problem Research
2. **Consultation 2:** Solution and Conclusion
3. **Consultation 3:** Final Draft

## Choosing a Topic

Groups should pick topics aligned to their diploma or personal area of interest — this helps facilitate research with a familiar industry/field and project motivation.

Topics must align with the **5 Pillars of Singapore's Green Plan 2030** (https://www.greenplan.gov.sg/). Each group within the same class must select a different topic.

Groups may propose a different Climate Change challenge topic or problem statement related to their diploma, subject to tutor's approval.

**Important:** No fundraising project should be considered.

### Available Climate Change Challenge Topics

- Circular Economy
- Liveable City and Community
- Green Buildings
- Renewable Energy
- Green Finance and Impact Investment
- Sustainable Food System / Food Security
- Sustainable Materials / Packaging
- Green Transportation
- Sustainable / Regenerative Tourism
- Green Economy Opportunities
- Waste Management & Recycling
- Biodiversity and Conservation

## Recommended Presentation Structure

1. Introduction to the Climate Change challenge / Problem Statement
2. Challenge / Problem Research
3. Proposed Solution with supporting deliverable
4. Conclusion
5. References (with declaration on Gen AI tools used)

## Required Deliverable Formats

The proposed solution must include a deliverable in **one** of the following forms:

- **Physical prototype or model** — to be brought to the class presentation
- **Digital prototype** — model drawings, app mock-ups, or website mock-ups
- **Collateral material of a campaign** — examples include 3 sets of sample social media posts, a 1-minute video, or a 3-minute podcast

## Section Guidelines

### Introduction (3–5 slides recommended)

- Briefly introduce the Climate Change challenge / Problem Statement.
- Define the challenge or problem.
- Explain why it is important to Singapore.
- Explain why the group picked this topic or their interest.
- Include supporting pictures or videos.

### Research (5–8 slides recommended)

- Report research-based findings only — no opinions in this section.
- Must be relevant to Singapore (overseas examples must be justified for relevance).
- State current issues and unsustainable practices related to the challenge.
- Provide details on causes, effects, and impacts.
- Identify Singapore's goals related to the challenge.
- Identify stakeholders and target audience for the solution (e.g. TP, TP students, companies, agencies, or specific groups in society).
- Include statistics, pictures, infographics, or videos.

### Proposed Solution (8 or more slides recommended)

- Propose solution(s) or social campaign to address the challenge.
- Solutions can be improved versions of existing ideas or new concepts.
- For campaigns: identify a knowledge gap, include a call to action, and develop collateral materials.
- Describe how the solution will be implemented.
- Include the deliverable (physical prototype, digital prototype, or campaign collateral).
- Show that the solution is realistic and workable — discuss resources required and feasibility.
- Evaluate impact and success: discuss challenges, define a clear target/goal, and suggest how success will be measured (reach, engagement, participation, etc.).

### Conclusion (5 or more slides recommended)

- Wrap up the project. If goals were achieved, what other issues could be addressed next?
- Suggest next steps or follow-up actions.
- End with a key takeaway or message for the target audience.
- Each contributing group member gives a brief personal reflection: what was interesting/challenging, what was learned.

### References

- Apply APA or IEEE standards as per diploma requirements.
- Describe how Gen AI tools were used in the project.
- Share links or screenshots of conversations with Gen AI tools (prompts and responses).

## Grading Criteria

### Group Component (30%)

- **Research and analysis of problem (10%)** — clarity, accuracy, thoroughness, depth of understanding
- **Creativity / innovation in proposed solution (10%)** — design creativity, feasibility, practical implementation
- **Quality of deliverable (10%)** — professionalism, attention to detail, exhibition/competition-worthy quality

### Individual Component (10%)

- **Individual Presentation (including Q&A)** — preparation, confidence, eye contact, body posture, clarity, ability to elaborate, and ability to answer questions confidently.

### Important Notes

- If a student is determined by the tutor as a non-contributor or contributed little, that student will be required to do the project individually.
- Failure to submit or meet passing requirements results in a Fail grade.
- Good group projects may be selected for sustainability competitions or exhibitions inside or outside TP.

## Declaration of Originality

Groups must declare that the submission is original, that nothing has been "cut and pasted" directly from the internet, and that all original sources (including any use of Gen AI tools) have been appropriately acknowledged. Plagiarism — taking and using all or part of another person's work, including AI-generated work, and presenting it as one's own — carries TP's standard penalties.
